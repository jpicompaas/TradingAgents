Algorithms & references

What models this repo uses, why they were chosen, and the papers behind them.

1 · Stock-price forecasting (GARCH(1,1)-t Monte Carlo)

Each run produces three forward-looking price paths — headwinds, same, and tailwinds — which are now the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles of a 5,000-path Monte Carlo simulation driven by a fitted GARCH(1,1) with Student-t innovations. Inner-percentile windows form the uncertainty bands (e.g. headwinds low = P2.5, headwinds high = P25).

Why GARCH and not a constant-σ envelope?

The previous model assumed today's realized volatility was fixed for the next 90 days and wrapped a deterministic today × exp(±k·σ·√t) envelope around it. The single most robust stylized fact about equity returns is that volatility clusters — quiet periods follow quiet periods, shocks follow shocks (Cont, 2001). GARCH directly models that clustering, which means the bear case after a shock is wider than the bear case in calm regimes, and the model knows it.

Student-t innovations (Bollerslev, 1987) give the bear path realistic fat tails that a Gaussian model under-represents. Optional GJR-GARCH (Glosten, Jagannathan & Runkle, 1993) layered behind the GARCH_LEVERAGE=1 env switch captures the leverage effect — equities drop faster than they recover.

Pipeline
  1. Fetch ~1y of daily OHLCV from yfinance.
  2. Fit GARCH(1,1)-t on log returns scaled to percent (Sheppard's arch package).
  3. Forecast 90 days of conditional variance via Monte Carlo simulation (method='simulation', simulations=5000).
  4. Optional drift adjustment: when the Portfolio Manager's expected at +90d is within ±50% of today's close, shift the simulated returns by a constant δ so the median path tracks the LLM's central view (preserves the prior "same = LLM-anchored" behaviour).
  5. Compute per-day percentiles and emit forecast.csv + forecast.png.

References

  1. Bollerslev, T. (1986). Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity. Journal of Econometrics, 31(3), 307–327. DOI  — the original GARCH paper.
  2. Engle, R. F. (1982). Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity with Estimates of the Variance of United Kingdom Inflation. Econometrica, 50(4), 987–1007. DOI  — ARCH, Engle's Nobel-cited foundation paper.
  3. Bollerslev, T. (1987). A Conditionally Heteroskedastic Time Series Model for Speculative Prices and Rates of Return. Review of Economics and Statistics, 69(3), 542–547. DOI  — GARCH with Student-t innovations.
  4. Glosten, L. R., Jagannathan, R., & Runkle, D. E. (1993). On the Relation between the Expected Value and the Volatility of the Nominal Excess Return on Stocks. Journal of Finance, 48(5), 1779–1801. DOI  — GJR-GARCH (leverage-effect / asymmetric vol).
  5. Cont, R. (2001). Empirical Properties of Asset Returns: Stylized Facts and Statistical Issues. Quantitative Finance, 1(2), 223–236. DOI  — the canonical "stylized facts" reference; justifies why volatility clustering is the right thing to model.
  6. Andersen, T. G., Bollerslev, T., Diebold, F. X., & Labys, P. (2001). The Distribution of Realized Exchange Rate Volatility. Journal of the American Statistical Association, 96(453), 42–55. JSTOR  — foundational realized-volatility paper, the basis for the σ estimate that anchors the model.

Approaches considered and not adopted

  • Heston stochastic-volatility model (Heston 1993): well-cited and richer than GARCH, but its calibration is finicky on a single ticker and at 90-day horizons the marginal accuracy is small relative to the implementation cost.
  • N-BEATS (Oreshkin et al. 2019), N-HiTS (Challu et al. 2022), PatchTST (Nie et al. 2022): strong on M3/M4 benchmarks, but training on a single ticker overfits and CPU-only inference is sluggish. Pretrained foundation models like TimesFM (Das et al. 2023) and Chronos (Ansari et al. 2024) are interesting next-step candidates but pull in 200MB+ checkpoints and a much larger dependency tree — disproportionate for replacing an envelope formula.
  • Bai & Perron structural-change tests (Bai & Perron 2003): excellent for regime detection but doesn't directly produce a forward path distribution.

2 · Multi-agent debate framework

The agent topology (analysts → researchers → trader → risk debators → portfolio manager) is built on LangGraph, with a per-ticker SQLite checkpointer for crash-resume. The debate / role-play pattern is informed by the broader literature on LLM multi-agent systems:

  1. Du, Y., Li, S., Torralba, A., Tenenbaum, J. B., & Mordatch, I. (2023). Improving Factuality and Reasoning in Language Models through Multiagent Debate. arXiv:2305.14325. — evidence that adversarial multi-agent debate (the bull/bear, plus the risk debators) improves factual reasoning over single-agent prompting.
  2. Hong, S., et al. (2023). MetaGPT: Meta Programming for a Multi-Agent Collaborative Framework. arXiv:2308.00352. — role-specialized agents with hand-off contracts, mirrored by this repo's analyst → researcher → trader → manager flow.
  3. Wu, Q., et al. (2023). AutoGen: Enabling Next-Gen LLM Applications via Multi-Agent Conversation. arXiv:2308.08155. — general framework for tool-using LLM agents in conversation.

3 · Per-ticker scenario factor probabilities

After the run completes, a single structured-output call synthesises a ScenarioBreakdown — 3-6 ticker-specific factors per scenario, each tagged with an independent probability and an impact bucket. The schema enforces "concrete and specific" in the field descriptions; the structured-output mechanism prevents the model from skipping fields or returning prose.

  1. Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases. Science, 185(4157), 1124–1131. DOI. — the canonical reference on subjective-probability elicitation; treating LLM-emitted percentages as judgmental probabilities follows this tradition. Each factor's probability is independent (no implicit Σ=100).
  2. Tetlock, P. E. (2005). Expert Political Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know?. Princeton University Press. — motivates the explicit-probability format over vague "likely / probably" language, which is the convention the scenario card UI follows.

4 · Peer-comparable valuation

The Bull and Bear researchers receive a real peer PE / Forward PE table pulled live from yfinance (see tradingagents/dataflows/peers.py). This grounds their relative-valuation arguments in current numbers rather than the LLM's training-data recall.

  1. Liu, J., Nissim, D., & Thomas, J. (2002). Equity Valuation Using Multiples. Journal of Accounting Research, 40(1), 135–172. DOI. — evidence that forward-earnings multiples beat trailing-earnings multiples for cross-sectional valuation.
